ūüéĮ Core Concepts
The Nature of Control

Controls are the actions, policies, and procedures implemented by management and the board to manage risk and increase the likelihood of achieving organizational goals. They help ensure data accuracy, safeguard assets, prevent fraud, and ensure compliance.

The Control Environment

The control environment is the foundation of all control processes, encompassing the "tone at the top", management's philosophy, organizational structure, and the organization's integrity and ethical values.

The Control Process Cycle

Effective control requires continuous feedback: establishing standards ‚Üí measuring performance ‚Üí analyzing deviations ‚Üí taking corrective action ‚Üí reappraising the original standards.

Roles and Responsibilities

Senior management oversees the establishment of controls; operational managers actively assess them in their areas. Internal auditors do not create controls ‚ÄĒ they provide independent and objective assurance on whether controls are effective and efficient.

Automated vs. Manual Processing

Computer-based systems process transactions uniformly ‚ÄĒ eliminating random clerical errors, but guaranteeing that a programming flaw processes all similar transactions incorrectly. Automated systems often consolidate tasks, which can weaken traditional segregation of duties and require alternative control strategies.

ūüďĆ Key Terms & Definitions

Control Processes ‚ÄĒ Specific activities designed and operated to keep risks contained within an organization's accepted risk tolerance.

Risk and Control Matrix ‚ÄĒ A tool that helps auditors link business objectives, potential risks, and specific control processes to evaluate their design and effectiveness.

Audit Trail ‚ÄĒ A record allowing financial data to be traced back to its source; in IT systems this trail may only exist in computer-readable formats or for a very short duration.

Frequently Tested

Inherent limitations of internal control: controls provide reasonable assurance only ‚ÄĒ never absolute assurance. Limited by human mistakes, management override, collusion, and the cost of implementation.

Tricky Questions

When to use manual vs. automated controls. Manual = large, unusual, nonrecurring transactions or situations requiring judgment. Automated = high-volume routine transactions requiring strict mathematical accuracy.

Common Wrong Assumption

IT systems do NOT inherently improve segregation of duties. They often concentrate duties into the hands of anyone with access ‚ÄĒ significantly increasing unauthorized data alteration risk if not properly monitored.

ūüéĮ Core Concepts
Primary vs. Secondary Controls

Primary controls (preventive, detective, corrective, and directive) are key measures designed to manage risks directly. Secondary controls (compensating and complementary) provide additional layers of protection when primary controls are missing or ineffective.

Control Levels

Controls are applied at three levels: Entity level (board governance and management oversight); Process level (specific business unit objectives); Transaction level (specific application rules or segregation of duties).

IT General vs. Application Controls

IT General Controls (ITGCs) form the umbrella over the entire processing environment ‚ÄĒ data center security, system development, access management. Application controls are built into specific software programs (like payroll) to ensure complete and accurate input, processing, and output of data.

Time-Based Controls

Feedback controls ‚ÄĒ learn from past mistakes. Concurrent controls ‚ÄĒ monitor and adjust ongoing processes in real-time. Feedforward controls ‚ÄĒ anticipate and prevent long-term future problems.

ūüďĆ Key Terms & Definitions

Compensating Control ‚ÄĒ A mitigative measure (like increased supervision) used when standard segregation of duties is not possible; does NOT reduce risk to an acceptable level by itself.

Hash Total ‚ÄĒ A batch input control that sums numerical data without a defined meaning (e.g., list of invoice numbers) strictly to verify all data was processed.

Validity Check ‚ÄĒ An online input control that compares entered data against a table of valid values (e.g., matching a vendor number against an approved vendor list).

Directive Controls ‚ÄĒ Primary controls that encourage a desirable event, such as employee training or policy manuals.

‚ö†ÔłŹ Exam Traps & Frequently Tested
Frequently Tested

IT General Controls vs. IT Application Controls: Physical security / password restrictions for the overall network = General. A rule restricting data entry in a specific payroll screen = Application.

Tricky Questions

Data input controls: Batch processing ‚Üí financial totals, record counts, hash totals. Online (real-time) processing ‚Üí limits, ranges, check digits, validity checks, field/format checks.

Common Wrong Assumption

One control does NOT map perfectly to one risk. A Control Matrix reveals that multiple controls may address a single risk, or one control may address several risks.

‚úÖ Detective controls = alert proper people AFTER an unwanted event occurs (not before).
‚úÖ Catching data entry typos in real-time (e.g., a letter typed in a numeric field)? ‚Üí Look for "Field or format checks".
‚úÖ "Memo Posting" = bank intermediate step to temporarily show account updates before actual end-of-day batch processing is complete.
